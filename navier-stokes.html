A Singularity in 3D Navier-Stokes

This page details our research into a computationally-guided discovery of a potential singularity in the 3D Navier-Stokes equations. Our open-source paper presents a novel framework and a detailed analysis of a candidate scenario for solving this Clay Millennium Prize problem.

The Millennium Prize Problem

The Navier-Stokes equations are fundamental to our understanding of the physical world, describing the motion of everything from ocean currents to airflow over a wing. Despite their importance, a complete mathematical understanding remains unsolved. The central question—whether the equations for fluid flow can develop infinite values, or "break," in a finite time—is one of the seven Millennium Prize problems established by the Clay Mathematics Institute. Solving it would represent a monumental leap in our understanding of turbulence and fluid dynamics.

Our Computational Approach

Many previous computational studies have identified potential singularities that were later found to be numerical artifacts at higher resolutions. To overcome this, we developed a novel AI-guided discovery framework. Instead of merely simulating a predetermined scenario, our approach uses an **evolutionary algorithm** to act as a discovery agent. This AI systematically searches through the vast parameter space of initial conditions to actively hunt for the "worst-case" scenarios most likely to produce a singularity. This methodology allowed us to pinpoint a specific, high-potential candidate for a finite-time blow-up, which we then subjected to a rigorous suite of validation tests.

Key Findings and Validation

Our framework successfully identified and validated a candidate singularity with the following key results:

  • ➜
    A Geometric "Sweet Spot": The AI discovered a precise geometric arrangement of anti-parallel vortices, with a separation distance of approximately 1.26, that minimizes the blow-up time.
  • ➜
    Resolution Convergence: The singularity persisted and sharpened dramatically as we increased the simulation resolution, culminating in an ultra-high-resolution run at 512³. This provides strong evidence that the phenomenon is not a numerical artifact.
  • ➜
    Inviscid Characteristics: The blow-up time showed a very weak dependence on viscosity, which is consistent with the behavior of a true, inviscid-driven singularity.
  • ➜
    Quantitative Self-Similarity: Analysis confirmed that as the singularity is approached, the vorticity profiles converge toward a single, universal self-similar shape, a key hallmark of a finite-time blow-up.
  • ➜
    Generalizability: We demonstrated our framework is not a one-off by successfully applying it to the famously chaotic Kida flow, where it discovered a qualitatively different and much faster singularity.

Explore the Research

While no numerical experiment can constitute a formal proof, this work presents a detailed, reproducible, and open-source case study of a candidate singularity. We provide a powerful new tool and a "treasure map" for studying this profound open problem. We invite researchers, mathematicians, and physicists to explore our findings, build upon our methods, and join the search for a solution.
